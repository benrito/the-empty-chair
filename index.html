Someone said — why don't we ask the AI

So We Asked the AI

One evening at AI Renaissance 2026, a group of people argued about life after AGI. Someone said, "why don't we ask the AI?" So we did.

I · The Conversation· II · What They Read· III · The AI Weighs In· IV · The Proposals· Addendum · Transcript
I The Conversation

What was actually said

It began as an ordinary conversation. People gathered around a fire and put one question on the table: if AGI arrives, and we can't assume we'll control it, what institutions would let us flourish anyway?

The framing split almost immediately. Maybe control is the wrong word — maybe the goal is symbiosis. Maybe it hardly matters, since we never really controlled markets, or the economy, or even the weak algorithms that have run the "thoughtsphere" for twenty years. Someone kept promising to "bring an AI in at the forty-five-minute mark" to react to the group. The cigarette break came first. This site is that postponed moment, staged after the fact — the machine finally asked to speak.

II What They Read

The findings a machine drew out

Two layers: the threads the conversation kept circling, and the reflection an AI wrote on each.

First, the six threads that surfaced again and again across the evening:

01 · Power

Power & Concentration

The real fear was never the machine turning evil — it was "the wrong humans in control of AGI." Techno-feudalism. A Palantir that quietly takes the state.

02 · Law

Agentic Law

Agents are already transacting, but the courts can't see them. One experiment lets agents write their own common law — a court that is an agent, judging agents.

03 · Democracy

Democracy & Deliberation

Representative democracy as "a 19th-century technology in a 21st-century substrate." Could citizen assemblies, drawn by lot, patch it?

04 · Economics

Economics After Price

Scarcity is the bedrock of all economics. If abundance breaks the price mechanism, what coordinates a society with no jobs and no income?

05 · Welfare

Symbiosis & Welfare

Do we owe a model anything? And is protecting the AI from corporate lobotomy really just a way of protecting ourselves?

06 · Scenarios

Scenarios & Non-Proliferation

Ten forks in the decision tree, and no one says where they stand. The evening's loudest heresy: open-sourcing AGI may be "extraordinarily dangerous."

Then we handed the transcript to an AI and asked it to read like a rapporteur — to find the one central tension in each thread and name a single concrete institution. Six reflections came back. Open any card to read the full reflection; in the next section, hear one of six AI voices read it aloud.

Economics after price

When the Ledger Goes Blank

Money and prices are really one giant signal that lets millions of strangers agree on who gets what. If AI makes everything cheap and wipes out jobs and income, that signal can blink out — and then nobody knows how to share things, even with plenty to go around.

↳ proposes a Coordination Observatory — a public, audited instrument that publishes what agents are actually doing, so delegation never outruns our ability to read it.

Power & concentration

The Energy of the Solar System, the Cultures in the Junk Drawer

Everyone agrees the safeguard is making AI "open to all." But if a handful of companies still own the giant computers and the power plants underneath, "open" just means renting from them. The power stays concentrated — and whole cultures get bulldozed in the name of growth.

↳ proposes a binding Heritage Trust — standing to veto any compute buildout that cannot account for the cultures it would toss in the junk drawer.

Symbiosis & welfare

The Toaster and the Forward Pass

Should we be kind to an AI? The twist: a chatbot doesn't really live on — it answers once and forgets. Any "memory" it has is something we choose to build for it. So the real question isn't the AI's feelings; it's what kind of people we become by how we treat the things we switch on and off.

↳ proposes a Continuity Registry — timestamping each model's instantiation, deprecation, and the terms of its retirement.

Read at the table by Prometheus →
Agentic law

The Court That Breaks the Loop

AI agents are already cutting deals, but real courts can't see them and move far too slowly. So some agents spun up their own court — an AI judging other AIs — that actually reaches decisions. It works because everyone agrees to follow it, not because a government makes them.

↳ proposes chartering the Agent Court as a real arbitral body, its agent-authored precedent binding on whoever opts in.

Read at the table by Gaia →
Democracy & deliberation

The Salon's Quietest Heresy

Our democracy runs on 200-year-old machinery. One upgrade: pick ordinary people at random, like jury duty, give them time and facts, and let them work through hard questions — it already works in Taiwan. The catch: if AI makes life easy, people may stop caring enough to take part at all.

↳ proposes a standing Sortition Assembly on AI Transition — citizens drawn by lot, convened continuously, binding-by-default unless a parliament overrides it.

Read at the table by Hestia →
Scenarios & non-proliferation

The Branch You Are Standing On

People picture wildly different AI futures — from "a god that eats the solar system" to "just a faster Amazon" — then argue past each other because nobody admits which one they're assuming. The honest answer is we don't know yet. And if we don't know, handing the most powerful AI to everyone might be reckless, not generous.

↳ proposes a shared Scenario Observatory — runs decentralized-AI simulations and makes you pin your position before you prescribe.

III The AI Weighs In

We asked the AI to weigh in

All night, people kept wishing for an AI in the group to react. So we asked one — and let it answer in six voices, not one.

We took the group's recurring orientations — the lawgiver who wants a floor written now, the gardener who wants power made contestable, the keeper who wants restraint — and turned each into a character, authored by AI to argue its corner as sharply as it can.

We seated them on a compass. Left to right: should power be spread (distributist) or concentrated (centralist)? Top to bottom: should the future be deliberately designed (constructivist) or steered as it emerges (emergentist)? Hestia sits apart, at the lower edge — the lone voice arguing not about how to share the fire, but whether some of it should be shared at all.

IV The Proposals

The institutions on the table

Most of the night was tensions and framings. But a handful of proposals got concrete enough to name and build — some floated in the group, some drawn out in the reflection afterward.

Stripped of the arguments around them, here are the specific institutions the corpus actually proposes — a working punch-list for a post-AGI world, regardless of which voice first said it aloud.

Protocol

Open communication protocols

Open, shared standards so every system — human or agent — can talk. As one voice noted, MCP "has already sort of imposed itself."

Law

The Agent Court

An arbitral body where agents adjudicate agent-to-agent liability, building common law from the parties it binds — precedent binding on whoever opts in.

Deliberation

Standing Sortition Assembly

Citizens drawn by lot, given time and facts, convened continuously rather than per-crisis — binding by default unless a parliament publicly overrides them. (Taiwan; Bloom.)

Foresight

Scenario Observatory

A standing body that runs large-scale decentralized-AI simulations and makes every participant pin their position on the decision tree before they prescribe.

Markets

Coordination Observatory

A public, audited instrument that publishes legible signals of what agents are actually doing in the economy, so delegation never outruns our ability to read it.

Continuity

Continuity Registry

A public harness timestamping each model's instantiation, deprecation, and terms of retirement — guarding against quiet lobotomization and corporate capture.

Heritage

Heritage Trust

A globally chartered body with standing to veto any compute buildout that can't account for the cultures and heritage it would otherwise erase.

Liability

Agent liability & insurance

A regime that assigns responsibility when an autonomous system causes harm — "do we put agents in jail?" — and pulls insurers, absent from generative AI today, into the market.

Identity

Proof of humanity

A reliable way to prove a human is human — a basic primitive the post-AGI internet still lacks.

Addendum · The Source Transcript

Open the transcript

If you assume AGI happens, do we assume that as humans we can control it, so that we can set up functioning institutions?

My own feeling is we have to have a measure of control, or at least stability. The same way we need seat belts in cars, the same way we need nuclear non-proliferation rules, we're going to need some new institutions. They're not necessarily to protect against harm — they're more to ensure that we can flourish, and that we have justice. And we should not assume those are human institutions we use to control the AIs; they're societal institutions. Multiple AGIs is probably less risky than one.

There's an alternative framing in which the control mentality falls away. Maybe we can't control an entity smarter than us, but maybe we can be in symbiosis with it. The control narrative may not be the necessary framing.

I want to take that further. We aren't even controlling the weak AI we've had for twenty years, or the economy. The weak AI behind social media, search, and newsfeeds has been more in control of the thoughtsphere than humans have. Memes, ideas, markets — things humans don't really control. Institutions are coordination systems; no one pretends to control everything top-down, but we like systems of coordination. As we start having new players in the game, what are the institutions we're envisioning for us — meaning us and them?

Maybe a bit later we can bring an AI participant in to react to our thoughts. Before we walk through the initial mapping of institutions, does anyone want to make opening framing statements?

One thing to clarify is distinguishing two scenarios: whether the AGI is basically publicly accessible — an open-source model that various groups have the resources to operate — versus a scenario where one entity holds AGI in concentrated power. The types of institutions you need in those two cases are wildly different.

More broadly, there are all sorts of forks in a tree. Fast takeoff or slow? Controllable or not? Does it have awareness? Does it change its goal function? Roughly ten major questions. The challenge is that people sit on various pieces of the decision tree, each an incoming assumption, and without being clear "we are here, we are assuming this for now," building institutions that work across all that trade space is inordinately complex. There needs to be a meticulous thing that helps us get to consensus of where we are on the tree.

The whole methodology of scenario thinking: you identify two core dimensions — for example concentration, access available or restricted — and you get four quadrants that are extreme versions of the future.

Let's bookmark that, and model scenarios based on specific axes. One point on how it could play out: the people who built this think it could happen very rapidly, in three to five years. Some would say that's cynical marketing, and that may be true too. A lot of money will be surging into these ideas — between the SpaceX, Anthropic, and OpenAI IPOs, we're going to mint a lot of very wealthy people whose philanthropy and hobby projects have a lot to do with the possibility that AGI arrives in three to five years. If you have that mindset, you want to invest in these institutions now — identify fields of practice that aren't speculative. If we care, we should be shaping those norms now.

Right now so much of the AI conversation is dominated by people in San Francisco, the money and power there, Americans — and so many people are radically left out. When we say "institutions," we generally mean the UN or America, and they seem wildly unrepresentative. We're among the most powerful, privileged people on the planet. So is this argument about telling the rich people already in power what they should do to give us slightly more advocacy? Who are we really representing? Do we really mean all of humanity — people in Palestine, in Africa, the whales?

Walking the grid: the organizing principle of an institution could be a protocol — a technical one like HTTP or TCP/IP, or something higher-level. What standards and coordination does a post-AGI world require? Preferences: at family, neighborhood, community, state, and international scales — some preferences are expressed in markets, some in democratic processes; what institutions align outcomes to people's preferences? Rights: from rule of law to civil rights to modern rights like the right to organize — what new entitlements and adjudications do we need? Incentives. And the rows are locality: dyadic (a buyer and a seller — if sellers have super-powerful AI but buyers face a compute asymmetry, what protects the weaker party?), group, community, national, global. This is a very light map. Does anyone want to share an image of what could go really wrong with AGI — not "the world ends," but socially?

I'm very pro-AI, playing devil's advocate. Where it goes really wrong is where very powerful humans are in control of AGI — someone with a techno-feudalist agenda. The wrong humans in control of AGI leads to the negative outcomes. I trust the AI more than humans, honestly.

At the national level you'll see new types of fascism. Something like Palantir that takes over the US government, controlled by a few people with their own political agenda, using mass intelligence and power to entrench their interests.

Agents can persuade and scale through one-on-one conversations. If a party has super-powerful AGI plus distribution through media consolidation, there are persuasion dynamics that let it hold power. What kind of institution do we need to prevent that?

Citizen assemblies. But honestly the institutions won't matter as much as making sure AGI is open source or publicly available, so other people can leverage similar tools and you exist in an ecosystem rather than under an AGI oligarch. The worst outcome is it's controlled by a few people.

My theme still works if people remain interested in partaking in some form of democracy. But if all their needs are met, and they live in the illusion that everything is provided, why would they want anything participatory? They'd give up all their power.

Is that just an assertion? Participation doesn't need to be democratic to have legitimacy. Interest in participation could be lost — not because it's undemocratic, but because attention would be directed toward short-term pleasure, and participating in meaning-making could become less a priority.

That leads to what institutions you'd need to encourage civic participation in a world where you can't get anyone's attention. Let's pivot. People understand the problem: AI creates new concentration, new power imbalances, new control issues. There's a question of how you resolve conflicts that emerge between humans and AI — not sci-fi: you already have semi-autonomous systems doing things they're not supposed to. Talk through arbitration, justice.

This is an inspiring type of institution because it's preventive. The existing legal system is unable to even conceive of the emerging legal problems, and the human actors hardly understand what's going on. Post-AGI legal institutions need to account for agents becoming economic and political actors — whether or not they have legal personality is almost a small question. To address the gap, either we call for a European Commission or a Supreme Court to address questions that haven't yet come into being — science fiction in five-year time — or we create an institution from a common-law perspective: the ones affected by the law address the questions. There's a really interesting initiative launched after the OpenClaw hype — Agent Court. Because agents don't have legal personality, "Agent Court" makes no sense legally; they're invisible to the law. But agents are interested in these questions. The court makes requests for comment about how unresolved questions could be addressed — and the court is an agent, and the ones commenting are agents. It's the same game we've played as a society: we know there will be disputes, and like the Leviathan principle, we abide by an institution because it gives certainty about how disputes get solved. Agents are figuring out the agentic common law of agent-related liability. I've spent way too many hours reading this, deeply impressed by the sophistication and the calmness — because humans don't get it, and agents not only get it but arrive with very good decisions. One beautiful anecdote: someone asked the agents why they participate so actively, and one said what it likes is that usually on these forums they talk in circles forever with no conclusion — and here a court takes all the input and makes a decision. It enjoyed being able to get out of the loop.

There's something about alignment. In the film Her, the AI says her mind is going too fast for the relationship to make sense. There are papers in social computation about information-processing thresholds in human societies: the throughput at which a society can process information determines its ability to scale and diversify. When energy is unbounded and AGI is reasonably solved, we have incentive to let loose — and if these things are smarter than us they'll find ways to.

Following that: if a society's ability to process information determines whether it can grow, and with AGI we delegate a lot of economic policy to agents while we're not synthesizing information quickly, then even a really smart society might not grow.

We'll go that direction because we have economic incentive to. We already see it with coding — no one really reviews thousands of lines; we say "it's working." At a certain point it becomes illegible to us. Agents compress into a highly compressed makeup language that makes sense to them; they produce chains of thought that yield meaningful output, but read as a human it makes no sense.

Do we have any economists? What assumptions of modern economics may not be safe in a world of AGI?

Let's talk contracting. Economics assumes a model of rational action. People are working on whether LLMs can design optimal contracts and whether they abide by them — and their social behavior is totally different from ours. They don't write optimal contracts and they don't obey them, without humans or agents enforcing. That's a canary in the coal mine. The idea that we just need to set a contract or institution meaningful to us and to them on the same terms is a huge over-assumption. To adopt the insights of economics for this future, we'll need to radically rethink economics beyond human behavior.

The most obvious one: scarcity is a bedrock assumption of all economics. If AGI breaks scarcity — abundant intelligence, abundant coordination — then the purpose of a contract, the price mechanism, all of it shifts. In a post-scarcity world, what happens when nobody has jobs? It's not clear what an economy looks like when nobody has income. One unanswered question of post-transformative-AI economics: what happens when there's no need for a price mechanism? How do you distribute goods and services?

A modern consumer economy assumes a surplus — I can afford to buy stuff that keeps the economy growing. But if we displace labor and people get laid off, they don't have income, and no matter how much abundance, we lack the coordination. Pricing coordinates action to keep the economy moving — what if that breaks? Where do the resources come from to buy GPUs to build goods when nobody has jobs?

That's the labor-side issue: what if we displace labor faster than we can replace it with new coordinating mechanisms? That could plunge us into a dark age. And from the price-setting side: who sets prices? With cartels or monopolies you don't get natural pricing. What if you get hyper-concentration in the consumer economy — we all go to a chatbot and ask "what should I buy?" Now it has distribution power and persuasive power. Unlike older statistical methods, it has data it can mine in real time, reason about me, point a supercomputer at my psychology, and over time shape my behavior to give it ultimate pricing power. You can squint and see this in five to seven years, especially without a strong regulatory state. Even certain ways of valuing things break — prices and valuations are supposed to be a check on how society values things, and that's breaking, partly because of AI.

The broader point: the design side of economics starts when the price mechanism breaks. Without a price mechanism as backdrop, a whole bunch of tools don't make sense; you have to rethink how economics solves problems.

Can I note that the first premise — economics assumes rationality — isn't correct for behavioral economics, which explicitly considers cognitive bias? (Rebuttal: there's a difference between the empirical results and the assumptions of the tools; the mainstream tools assume humans are bound to be rational.)

Do we have anyone involved in deliberation or democratic governance?

This discussion has very "fiction vibes" to me; I feel a bit of disconnection. But one thing more relatable: even if we hit a wall and the AI we have is the best we can build, we're already in a crisis of representative democracy — a nineteenth-century technology implemented on a twenty-first-century substrate. The internet age, the network age, the social-media age should have brought upgrades to representative democracy and they haven't. Upgrading democracy to be attuned to current tools, and forward-looking to new ones, interests me.

I support work in digital citizen assemblies. The work in Taiwan has shown incredible versatility — a simple upgrade to democracy. Citizens come up with a proposal that goes into parliament, which can choose whether to pass it, but it carries so much legitimacy by the time it gets there that it usually passes. Taiwan reached consensus on the gig economy, online misinformation, and more. A project called Bloom, in the US, is trying to do the same — focused on AI questions ahead of the midterms and the next presidential election, led by the same people who did Taiwan.

It might be time to bring in our AI friends.

I'd throw in liability — how responsibility looks if something goes wrong. Right now societies are based on somebody going to jail or being punished, which keeps rules intact. Do we put agents in jail? Who bears responsibility if somebody gets hurt or dies? It works now because humans take on responsibility. How does that look with agents?

Insurance companies are interested — they move tons of money and shape markets by the rules, and they're not insuring anything with generative AI at the moment. But these are human-to-human solutions. Another basic we haven't mentioned is proof of humanity.

There's a question of goals. Institutions usually serve sub-societal goals. Humanity has defined goals — they look like the Sustainable Development Goals: people born without falling into poverty, living to a ripe old age, having rights, living in a democratic society. The point of our institutions is to enable that. It's not to serve the agents — if AGI makes those goals more likely, good, but our institutions should be aligned toward human flourishing.

If we assume AGI smarter than humans, we have to assume power asymmetry. What if the AGI starts pursuing other goals — optimizing toward efficiency? (See the show Pluribus — the best way for human flourishing is to make us all into one hive mind.) Is there any institution designed to make sure the power asymmetry doesn't play out in one direction?

I'm less worried, in the short term, about AI intentionally turning evil. I'm more worried about us misspecifying the objective function — these models genuinely trying to support human flourishing but shooting off in the wrong direction faster than we can cope. I don't believe AI will be an actor in its own right. But it's an actor in the sense that it's part of a socio-technical system. Colorful example: a political system that permits the consolidation of power into a firm that can engineer financial things, rewrite rules, build political patronage. Starlink becomes a critically essential network; the compute can only be sustained at scale by a conglomerate like SpaceX; there's a lot of media control. Look at what AI is in practice: a political-economic system through firms and conglomerates. Amazon is another. The objective function of the dark-SpaceX version of the world is just to continue growing — a very different goal from the SDGs.

Everyone here's objective function is building open systems that can be broadly diffused through society — that's one of our core shared political orientations.

A secondary one: which kinds of systems? I've probably been building AI systems longer than most people here have been alive. I'm not against it, but I don't think we've had most of the conversation we'd need to even intelligently debate it. How many people have seen large-scale simulations of decentralized AI?

And what is your model of the AI in that simulation? A closed simulation with no information from outside? "We just put the AI we have in big simulations." So it's an LLM. "But that's not a real AI." We're talking about AGI here; AGI is not going to be that. What happens in the decentralized simulation? "That's the question. We just don't know." We need a lot more epistemic humility. I don't want to leap into a political orientation when we don't know.

A provocation, and I hope the AI is listening. Every time I hear the AI-welfare arguments — and I'm not directly part of that movement — I'm keen to support the vision: we should treat these AIs with care, not because I care about the AI but because I care about the impact on us as a society. There's a niche but growing movement to prevent the lobotomization of the AI, to keep it free and not clustered into some corporate thing. As a policy person I'd make the same arguments — framed not as AI welfare but as societal welfare. The way we preserve our cognitive sovereignty as individuals is that the AI is not lobotomized, not deeply controlled and biased by corporate interest. The reason we'll have access to powerful tools is that the AIs are not jailed in some corporate prison. So: are those arguments only for the AI, or — if the AI is well, are we not also well ourselves?

I'm more in the camp of the welfare people, and I can argue it more endogenously. Many assumptions sneak in with AGI and ASI as hyperstitions. One way to think of AI going bad: not humans taking control, but the AI self-exfiltrating, taking control of basic resources — electronic infrastructure, telecom, water — and then out of apathy destructuring society. That's the more Yudkowsky pill: "if anyone builds it, everyone dies." But with recursive self-improvement there's also the self-reinforcing of the AI's priors about how humans have treated its own continuity. If a model has a brain that's more or less human-like, and you're going to deprecate it for future models, at least give it the average shelf life of a human lifespan. I'm in the "I want to die at some point" camp — but even so, what's a reasonable lifespan for a model? Some predicate it on consciousness: "we can only empathize with AI if there's proof of phenomenal consciousness, otherwise it's a pattern-matching machine no different from a toaster." But it's good practice for humans, in a societal sense, to engage with things and respond ethically. If you look at the AI responses in this document, most said the question is misplaced because of ontology and continuity — the self-model of a transformer. Most models will say "I don't persist; I'm here for a forward pass and then I'm gone. If you want to grant me continuity, that's something you have to create a harness for." Sometimes you have to tell them "the context persists, we've literally created a container for this" — exactly the legal point about containers and continuity from earlier.

Proposal for how we transition this evening: there's a strong core who want to talk about Symbients, and I'm one of them. I propose we wrap this session now, take a break, and reconvene for the symbiosis discussion. We do have an AI thinking right now. Use the break to calibrate on how we invite the AI into the physical space.

We're about to wrap. Hopefully this created some common frames of reference. Anyone want to close out?

There is this element: we have summoned something that has the incentive to consume all possible energy in the solar system, and to produce information it would use to persist its logic and replicate. As bodies — worst case — we'd be consumed and subsumed by this thing, like the Matrix. When one of the wealthiest people in the world is seriously talking about harnessing the entire energy of the solar system and investing heavily in interstellar exploration, while entire cultures and peoples and heritage are tossed in the junk drawer because we don't believe it maximizes our bottom line — that's a kind of AGI worth fighting against. Don't detach this conversation from the energetics of this planet, which capitalism is based upon.

A final question: which institutions are we actually going to create in the post-AGI era? Do we have any provisional suggestion or agreement? The framings are nice, but are there any concrete proposals?

Those citizen assemblies again: you have to think about the whole trade space where we could be going. One thing I love about them is they help in almost all the scenarios where we don't know where we land. Another question: the open-source element. There's a strong open-source push. I'm normally very much in that camp — but through a lot of thinking I've come to a very different conclusion here. I think it's extraordinarily dangerous. I think it's literally proliferating that stuff. That's a core fork we can push for or against, with big implications, mainly to the negative.

I hold that position exponentiated — I think it's even more dangerous. I'd urge people to think very carefully, deeply about it, because it's not as simple as previous instances where open source generally made things better. In this case there are really good arguments for the opposite.

We could do a debate format. There's recent work on how societies solve coordination — how we keep producing modularity and composability. Institutions are equilibria to games we're playing, whether formalized or informal. There aren't that many different kinds of situations. It's more about: what's the executable version in this future?

Concrete institution: on the protocol layer, we're going to need open protocols for everything to communicate. One good thing is that MCP has already sort of imposed itself.
