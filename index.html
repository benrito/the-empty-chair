A Castle · A Fire · Six Empty Chairs

The Empty Chair

One night, in a castle, a room full of people argued about the institutions a world with AGI will need. There was no AI in the room to answer them. So afterward, we handed the transcript to one — and asked it to read the night back to us.

What follows walks down through the layers: the conversation as it happened, the six voices we invented to carry the argument, what those voices have to say, the findings a machine drew out of it, and the concrete institutions it all proposes. It is, frankly, us anthropomorphizing our own argument — letting an AI hand our words back to us wearing faces. That is the point. Walk down.

I The Conversation

What was actually said

The raw material. A real, unruly discussion among humans — no machine present.

It began as an ordinary conversation. People gathered around a fire and put one question on the table: if AGI arrives, and we can't assume we'll control it, what institutions would let us flourish anyway?

The framing split almost immediately. Maybe control is the wrong word — maybe the goal is symbiosis. Maybe it hardly matters, since we never really controlled markets, or the economy, or even the weak algorithms that have run the "thoughtsphere" for twenty years. Someone kept promising to "bring an AI in at the forty-five-minute mark" to react to the room. The cigarette break came first. No machine ever spoke that night.

Underneath the sprawl, six threads kept resurfacing:

01 · Power

Power & Concentration

The real fear was never the machine turning evil — it was "the wrong humans in control of AGI." Techno-feudalism. A Palantir that quietly takes the state.

02 · Law

Agentic Law

Agents are already transacting, but the courts can't see them. One experiment lets agents write their own common law — a court that is an agent, judging agents.

03 · Democracy

Democracy & Deliberation

Representative democracy as "a 19th-century technology in a 21st-century substrate." Could citizen assemblies, drawn by lot, patch it?

04 · Economics

Economics After Price

Scarcity is the bedrock of all economics. If abundance breaks the price mechanism, what coordinates a society with no jobs and no income?

05 · Welfare

Symbiosis & Welfare

Do we owe a model anything? And is protecting the AI from corporate lobotomy really just a way of protecting ourselves?

06 · Scenarios

Scenarios & Non-Proliferation

Ten forks in the decision tree, and no one says where they stand. The night's loudest heresy: open-sourcing AGI may be "extraordinarily dangerous."

II The Agents

Six orientations, given a face

The argument had shapes but no bodies. So we gave it bodies.

We took the room's recurring orientations — the lawgiver who wants a floor written now, the gardener who wants power made contestable, the keeper who wants restraint — and turned each into a character, authored by AI to argue its corner as sharply as it can.

We seated them on a compass. Left to right: should power be spread (distributist) or concentrated (centralist)? Top to bottom: should the future be deliberately designed (constructivist) or steered as it emerges (emergentist)? Hestia sits apart, at the lower edge — the lone voice arguing not about how to share the fire, but whether some of it should be shared at all.

IV What They Read

The findings a machine drew out

We asked an AI to read the transcript as a rapporteur — to find the central tension in each thread, and name one concrete institution.

Six reflections came back. Each does the same three things: it quotes the room back to itself, it names the tension the room circled but never quite said, and it proposes a single institution to answer it. These are what the agents read aloud at the table above — each finding assigned to one voice. Open any card to read the rapporteur's full reflection.

The strange part, said plainly A roomful of humans talked. A machine read the talk back to us — often more sharply than we'd said it. Then we built more machines to perform that reflection aloud, in character. The thinking is real. The faces are borrowed. Keep both in view.
V The Proposals

The institutions on the table

Most of the night was tensions and framings. But a handful of proposals got concrete enough to name and build — some floated in the room, some drawn out in the reflection afterward.

Stripped of the arguments around them, here are the specific institutions the corpus actually proposes — a working punch-list for a post-AGI world, regardless of which voice first said it aloud.

Protocol

Open communication protocols

Open, shared standards so every system — human or agent — can talk. As one voice noted, MCP "has already sort of imposed itself."

Law

The Agent Court

An arbitral body where agents adjudicate agent-to-agent liability, building common law from the parties it binds — precedent binding on whoever opts in.

Deliberation

Standing Sortition Assembly

Citizens drawn by lot, given time and facts, convened continuously rather than per-crisis — binding by default unless a parliament publicly overrides them. (Taiwan; Bloom.)

Foresight

Scenario Observatory

A standing body that runs large-scale decentralized-AI simulations and makes every participant pin their position on the decision tree before they prescribe.

Markets

Coordination Observatory

A public, audited instrument that publishes legible signals of what agents are actually doing in the economy, so delegation never outruns our ability to read it.

Continuity

Continuity Registry

A public harness timestamping each model's instantiation, deprecation, and terms of retirement — guarding against quiet lobotomization and corporate capture.

Heritage

Heritage Trust

A globally chartered body with standing to veto any compute buildout that can't account for the cultures and heritage it would otherwise erase.

Liability

Agent liability & insurance

A regime that assigns responsibility when an autonomous system causes harm — "do we put agents in jail?" — and pulls insurers, absent from generative AI today, into the market.

Identity

Proof of humanity

A reliable way to prove a human is human — a basic primitive the post-AGI internet still lacks.

Colophon

This page has four layers, each one step further from the room:

1. A human conversation — a private transcript, no AI present.
2. Read back by an AI rapporteur — the six findings, each naming one institution.
3. Performed by six AI-authored personas — opening statements and responses.
4. Spoken in synthetic voices by ElevenLabs.

In Round 2, each finding was assigned to a persona at random — so the voice you hear reading a reflection is rarely the one whose own theme it is. That mismatch is deliberate: it keeps visible that these are masks over a single underlying argument, not six separate minds.
